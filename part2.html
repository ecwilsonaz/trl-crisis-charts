Diagnosis & Path Forward

Where the money went, how TRL compares to peers, and what the board can do about it

← Part 1: Anatomy of a Financial Crisis (Exhibits A–H) Part 2: Exhibits I–K
On March 27, 2026, Executive Director Cheryl Heywood resigned, effective June 30, relinquishing her duties immediately. At the same meeting, the board discussed launching a levy lid lift campaign — the first serious consideration of new revenue since 2009.

Together, the leadership transition and the fiscal crisis force a choice. Part 1 of this report documented how TRL arrived here — the widening gap between revenue and expenditures, the fund balance collapse, and the 61 frontline layoffs that followed. Part 2 asks three questions: where did the money go (Exhibit I), how does TRL compare to peers (Exhibit J), and what can the board do now (Exhibit K).
Key terms used in this report
FTE (Full-Time Equivalent)
A way of counting staff. One FTE = one full-time position. A half-time employee = 0.5 FTE.
Service Center
TRL's central administrative headquarters, which supports all 27 branch libraries. Includes HR, IT, Finance, Facilities, Creative Services, and Admin.
CPI (Consumer Price Index)
A standard measure of inflation — how much prices are rising. This report uses the Seattle-area CPI, the closest available measure for TRL's region.
Levy rate
The amount of property tax charged per $1,000 of your home's assessed value. TRL's rate of $0.224 means a homeowner with a $400,000 home pays about $90/year for library service.
Levy lid lift
A ballot measure that asks voters to raise the cap on how much property tax the library can collect. Washington law limits annual growth to 1%; a lid lift resets the rate higher.
Fund balance
The district's savings — money set aside from prior years to cover future expenses or emergencies.
TRL has two problems, not one. In 2024 (the latest year of state data), TRL spent $51 per resident on library service — less than every comparable peer except Fort Vancouver ($50). That's not a sign of efficiency. It's a sign of underfunding. TRL's levy rate ($0.224) is the lowest among all peers and less than half the statutory maximum. The last levy lid lift attempt was 2009. The result shows in the data: TRL delivers 9.6 circulations per resident, compared to 13–14 for better-funded systems like Sno-Isle, Whatcom, and KCLS.

And of the limited dollars TRL does collect, an increasing share goes to administration rather than library service. Between 2017 and 2026, the share of salary spending flowing to six administrative departments rose from 18.6% to 25.7% — while frontline staff were cut.

Not enough money coming in, and what does come in is increasingly going to the wrong place. The exhibits that follow diagnose both problems — and the board actions in Exhibit K address them in the right order: fix the allocation first, then fix the revenue.

A hiring freeze from January 2019 and COVID-era cuts drove Service Center FTE from 37 down to 29.5. By 2022, cumulative under-spending versus CPI had reached $1.6 million in savings. Then the curve reversed. In 2023, spending crossed above the CPI line for the first time. By 2026, the single-year gap was $1.3 million above CPI — and accelerating. At the current trajectory, 2027 will be worse.

The Rate: Per-Person Cost Growth

The average salary per full-time-equivalent position (FTE) in these six departments grew from $58,592 (2017) to $99,194 (2026) — a 69% increase. Seattle-area inflation over the same period was 43%. No peer library system grew faster than 27% from 2019 to 2024 (see Exhibit J).

Some of this growth was legitimate. TRL was the lowest-paid library system among its peers in 2019 (Exhibit J). A 2018–2019 pay and job-title review (known as a “classification and compensation study”) led to position reclassifications (changing job titles and pay grades) — department managers were retitled as directors and put at uniform salary levels. Some above-inflation growth was necessary catch-up. But the rate exceeded every comparable peer, and the growth was concentrated in administrative departments rather than frontline staff. Frontline workers also received pay adjustments — step increases and cost-of-living increases negotiated through collective bargaining — but those were contractual obligations agreed to by both parties, not discretionary decisions by leadership.

The Allocation: Who Got the Positions

From 2019 (pre-COVID) to 2026, TRL's Service Center added 8.4 FTE. Over the same period, the system as a whole lost 19.4 FTE. Non-Service-Center staff — the people who run the libraries — lost 27.7 positions. Service Center's share of total system FTE rose from 14.4% to 19.2%.

The clearest way to see the allocation shift: track Service Center's share of total salary spending over time. In 2017, these six departments consumed 18.6% of TRL's General Fund salary budget. By 2025, it had reached 22.7%. The 2026 figure — 25.7% — reflects the post-layoff budget: when 61 frontline positions are eliminated, the denominator shrinks and the administrative share rises mechanically. But the upward trend was already well established before the layoffs. The share was declining through 2020, then reversed sharply.

Service Center salary as a percentage of total General Fund salary expenditures (red line) and Service Center FTE as a percentage of total system FTE (blue line). The gap between the two lines reflects the fact that Service Center staff are paid more per person than frontline staff ($99K vs $68K in 2026). Both lines accelerate from 2022 onward. For comparison, KCLS (King County Library System) reports 31.5% of its total budget goes to administration — but that figure includes all department costs (IT infrastructure, facilities), not just salary.

When 61 frontline staff were laid off in March 2026 and zero administrative positions were eliminated, the pattern that had been building for years became visible to the public.

The Specific Choices

Three categories of decisions drove the acceleration:

Reclassifications and restructuring. In 2019, a classification and compensation study retitled department managers as directors and set uniform director-level salaries. In Administration, the Administrative Assistant and Administrative Support Services Manager roles were reclassified as Administrative Coordinator and Operations Coordinator, contributing to an 18.4% salary increase in that department for 2021 alone.

New positions created during declining reserves. The Employee Experience Advisor ($127K, created August 2024) and Special Projects Coordinator ($106K, created 2026) were added while the fund balance fell 60% and frontline layoffs were imminent. The Facilities+Courier department grew from 9.5 to 16.75 FTE — partly justified by the Mountain View library build and Expanded Access Hours infrastructure, but representing the single largest FTE increase of any department.

Executive compensation. The executive director's salary rose from $155K (2023) to $213K (2026) — a 37% increase in three years. A 2-year contract with 3% annual increases was approved December 30, 2025 — eleven weeks before the layoffs. Other senior administrative salaries followed a similar pattern: the HR Administrator rose 45% ($99K to $143K) and the Executive Administrator rose 49% ($90K to $134K) over the same period.

A note on Facilities + Courier. This department accounts for the largest FTE growth (12.0 → 16.75 from 2017) and the second-fastest per-person cost growth (+98%). Courier staff are represented by AFSCME Local 3758 — the same union as frontline library workers — and deliver materials between 27 branches. They are service-supporting rather than purely administrative. The Facilities expansion may reflect legitimate operational needs (the Mountain View library build, Expanded Access Hours infrastructure, and building maintenance for a growing number of facilities).

This exhibit includes Facilities+Courier because they are budgeted under Service Center operations and controlled by the same leadership team. But the analysis holds without them: excluding Facilities and Courier, the five core administrative departments (Admin, Creative Services, Finance, HR, IT) grew from $1.67M to $3.02M — an 81% increase, nearly double the 43% growth in Seattle-area inflation. The board should evaluate Facilities+Courier growth on its merits — some may be justified — but it should be evaluated, not assumed.

What This Does and Does Not Show

What this exhibit shows: The ten-year trajectory of salary spending in TRL's six Service Center departments, compared to Seattle-area inflation (CPI). It shows per-person cost growth, staffing levels, and the allocation of positions between Service Center and the rest of the system.

What this exhibit does not show: Whether individual positions or raises were justified. CPI is a benchmark for inflation, not for labor market competitiveness. TRL was the lowest-paid library system among its peers in 2019 (Exhibit J), and some above-CPI growth represented legitimate catch-up from that underpaid baseline. A 2018–2019 classification and compensation study drove some reclassifications. The relevant question is not “did costs grow faster than inflation?” — they did, and partly for defensible reasons — but “did costs grow faster than peers facing identical constraints?” Exhibit J answers that question: yes, at 1.3× the peer median rate — and concentrated in administration, not frontline.

Why no levy since 2009: This analysis documents that TRL's levy rate has declined 45% and that every comparable peer has pursued levy lid lifts during the same period. It does not address why TRL did not. The executive director's job description requires her to “explore and propose to the Board new potential sources of finance.” Whether that obligation was fulfilled — and whether the board was presented with levy options and chose not to pursue them — is a question that board meeting records could answer but this analysis does not attempt.

Board responsibility: Every budget containing these spending decisions was approved by the Board of Trustees. The allocation pattern documented here reflects both leadership recommendations and board votes. The “gap in information” described in Exhibit K (Step 8) may explain why the board did not intervene earlier, but it does not absolve the board of its oversight role. The governance reforms in Steps 7–10 are designed to prevent this pattern from recurring under any future executive director.

Benefits: Not included. Rising healthcare costs affect every public employer — this analysis isolates salary, the spending category most directly controlled by leadership decisions. A peer comparison of benefits-per-FTE growth (using the same state dataset) shows TRL's benefits growth (+30%, 2017–2024) was modestly above the peer median (+22%) but not an outlier; the salary growth pattern is the stronger anomaly. All salary figures reflect budgeted amounts from TRL Final Budget documents.

Sno-Isle: The Closest Peer Made Different Choices

Sno-Isle Libraries is TRL's closest structural peer — an intercounty rural library district, governed by a board of trustees appointed by county commissions, funded almost entirely by property taxes, subject to the same 1% annual cap on property tax growth imposed by state law (Initiative 747).

Sno-Isle's per-person salary growth was +28% from 2017 to 2024 — compared to TRL's +41%. Their 2025 cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) was 3.8% plus a 4.6% catch-up increase from the prior union contract. Transparent, negotiated, system-wide.

When Sno-Isle faced the same structural revenue constraint, their board adopted a resolution on March 23, 2026 to place a levy lid lift on the August 2026 ballot — asking voters to restore their rate to $0.47 per $1,000 of assessed value. Their 2018 levy passed by just 0.46% (50.46% yes). Snohomish County voted 52% against; Island County's 62% yes carried it. They're trying again rather than cutting frontline staff.

TRL's levy rate ($0.224) is less than half of Sno-Isle's target rate ($0.47) and less than half the statutory maximum ($0.50). TRL has not attempted a levy lid lift since 2009.

TRL's Frontline Staff Are Not Overpaid

At $64,269 salary per FTE (2024), TRL pays less than every peer system except Fort Vancouver ($58,367) and Whatcom County ($59,592). Per GovSalaries 2023 public records, TRL's median employee salary was $41,115 — 32% below Sno-Isle's median of $54,086. That median includes professional librarians and branch managers; classified bargaining-unit staff (library assistants, clerks, pages) typically earn $28,000–$35,000.

From 2017 to 2026, per-person costs in the six Service Center departments grew 69% ($58,592 to $99,194) while per-person costs for the rest of the system grew 55% ($44,023 to $68,288). But the sharper disparity is in headcount: the Service Center added 7.3 FTE (37.05 to 44.33) while the rest of the system lost 30.3 positions (216.4 to 186.1). The 2026 single-year gap between what Service Center departments actually spent and what they would have spent if costs had simply kept pace with inflation — $1.3 million — could fund roughly 14 fully loaded frontline positions (salary plus benefits at average system-wide cost), or about 19 at salary alone. In March 2026, 61 frontline staff were laid off.

The findings of Exhibit I apply to six specific administrative departments, not to the system as a whole. TRL's frontline workers were underpaid relative to peers before the layoffs. Any board action on salaries should increase frontline compensation toward peer levels, not reduce it.

Exhibit K

What the Board Can Do

The preceding exhibits diagnose the problem. This one proposes a response — ten actions, sequenced by timeline, designed to restore fiscal discipline, rebuild public trust, and preserve the five-county library district.

The sequencing matters. The board cannot credibly ask voters for more revenue (Step 5) until it has demonstrated fiscal discipline (Steps 1–4). Sno-Isle's 2018 levy passed by 0.46%. Public trust is the prerequisite for a levy lid lift — and trust must be earned through action, not promises.
Immediate — Next 90 Days
1
Freeze Service Center hiring
No new administrative positions until a staffing review is complete. Service Center FTE grew from 29.5 (2021) to 44.3 (2026) while 61 frontline staff were laid off. That sequence should not continue. Existing vacancies should be evaluated against operational need before being filled.
2
Commission a peer salary benchmarking study
Compare TRL's administrative compensation and staffing ratios to Sno-Isle, Fort Vancouver Regional, Pierce County, and Kitsap Regional — using the Washington State Library's annual statistical data (Exhibit J). Determine whether specific Service Center roles are above, at, or below peer market rates. Exhibit I shows spending grew faster than CPI; a benchmarking study shows whether it also exceeded market rate.
3
Commission an independent financial audit
The union, multiple community members, and former staff have called for this. The fund balance collapse (Exhibit C), the gap between leadership's public statements and the actual financial trajectory (Exhibit B), and the acceleration in Service Center spending documented in Exhibit I all warrant external review. The audit should examine whether financial information presented to the board was complete and accurate, and whether spending decisions followed board-approved policies.
Near-Term — Next 6–12 Months
4
Evaluate each Service Center position against operational need
Service Center FTE grew from 36 (pre-COVID) to 44.3 while the rest of the system lost 28 positions (Exhibit I). Some of those additions may be justified — IT staff for digital services, facilities workers for new buildings. Others should be evaluated for whether they serve an ongoing operational need or were discretionary additions during a period of declining reserves. Examples: the Employee Experience Advisor ($127K, created August 2024) and Special Projects Coordinator ($106K, created 2026) were added while reserves fell 60% and frontline layoffs were imminent. With the executive director position vacant, an external review is more credible than an internal one. Any savings identified should be directed toward restoring frontline service capacity. The Collective Bargaining Agreement includes recall provisions for laid-off employees.
5
Evaluate a levy lid lift
TRL's levy rate ($0.224 per $1,000 of assessed value — about $90/year for a $400,000 home) is less than half the statutory maximum ($0.50) and less than half of what Sno-Isle is asking voters to approve ($0.47). Under Washington law (RCW 84.55.050), TRL's board sends its levy lid request to the five county commissions for certification, then it goes to voters as a simple-majority ballot measure. A multi-year lid lift can span up to six years.

Every comparable peer system has used this tool. Sno-Isle: 2009, 2018, and now 2026. KCLS: 2002 and 2010. TRL has not tried since 2009. The 2009 attempt failed district-wide (45.1% yes) but passed in Mason County (55.4%) and Pacific County (51.8%) — the rural counties that would be most harmed by dissolution. Lewis County rejected it most strongly (29.2% yes).

The board should begin evaluating a levy immediately — the fiscal math demands it. But the ballot measure itself requires public trust, and trust requires visible action on Steps 1–4. Sno-Isle is targeting an August 2026 ballot. TRL cannot afford to wait years, but it also cannot credibly ask for more money without first demonstrating it will be spent differently.

6
Set compensation benchmarks for the next executive director
With the executive director's resignation, the board has a natural opportunity to reset. The outgoing ED's salary ($213K) was roughly proportional to peers by population served (Sno-Isle's ED: $244K for 800K population). The issue was not the level but the optics and timing — raises approved alongside frontline layoffs, and a 2-year contract with 3% annual increases adopted 11 weeks before the layoffs. The next ED contract should include performance metrics tied to fiscal health (reserve levels, administrative cost ratios), not just tenure-based increases.
Structural Reforms
7
Adopt an administrative cost ratio target
Set a board policy capping Service Center salary spending at a fixed percentage of total salary spending — for reference, the 2019 ratio was 17.6% of salary (14.4% of FTE). Review annually as part of budget approval. This makes the headcount inversion documented in Exhibit I visible in every future budget and prevents a repeat of the pattern where Service Center share of total FTE grew from 14.4% (2019) to 19.2% (2026) without board scrutiny.
8
Restore board financial oversight
The pattern documented in Exhibits A–C — leadership assuring the board of fiscal health while reserves collapsed — reflects a gap in information — leadership knew more than the board did. The board should require quarterly financial dashboards with trend lines (revenue vs. expenditures, fund balance trajectory, salary-per-FTE growth), not just annual budget presentations. Board members should have direct access to financial data, not solely leadership's interpretations of it.
9
Fill all board vacancies
Two of seven board seats — the Mason County seat and the Thurston County at-large seat — have been vacant since at least early 2025 (per board meeting minutes from January 2025 onward, which note “two positions that are vacant”). Every major financial decision of the crisis — the Collective Bargaining Agreement, the deficit budget, the executive director's contract, the layoffs — was made by a board operating at 5 of 7 members or fewer. The five county commissions that appoint TRL's trustees should fill all vacancies promptly. A full board is a prerequisite for the governance reforms described in Steps 7 and 8.
10
Preserve the five-county district
The 2009 levy vote showed that the two smallest rural counties — Mason (55.4% yes) and Pacific (51.8% yes) — voted to approve, while Thurston (46.5%), Grays Harbor (44.3%), and Lewis (29.2%) voted against. The measure failed district-wide at 45.1%. Dissolution would harm the communities that most support library funding and would eliminate the economies of scale that allow small branches in places like Amanda Park, Oakville, and Naselle to exist at all.

Administrative reform — not county exit — is the path that serves all five counties. The data in these exhibits shows that the deficit was caused by governance decisions, not by an inherent flaw in the intercounty model. The incoming executive director and the board can reverse those decisions. The model should be preserved.
The path forward in one sentence: demonstrate fiscal discipline through administrative reform (Steps 1–4), use the leadership transition to reset compensation expectations (Step 6), then ask voters for the revenue the district has been leaving on the table for seventeen years (Step 5), and build governance structures that prevent recurrence (Steps 7–10).

The data in these exhibits points to three conclusions: the direction of salary growth was defensible — TRL staff were underpaid. The speed was not sustainable — no peer system grew at anything close to TRL's rate. And the allocation favored administration over frontline service.

Right direction. Right speed. Right allocation.
