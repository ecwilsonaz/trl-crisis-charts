Timberland Regional Library:
Anatomy of a Financial Crisis

How leadership claimed financial health while fund balances collapsed, then laid off 61 frontline employees
Sources: TRL Final Budget documents (trl.org/budget), Board Meeting Minutes & Recordings — All public records

Timberland Regional Library serves five counties across rural western Washington — roughly 30 branches and 250 employees providing library services to half a million residents. In March 2026, TRL laid off 61 frontline staff, cut 38% of its frontline workforce, and converted three branches to unstaffed self-service. Leadership called it an unavoidable response to financial pressure.

Public budget documents and board meeting recordings tell a different story. The eight exhibits below trace, year by year, how the district spent down its reserves, who benefited, and what options were never pursued.

Exhibit C

Fund Balance Collapse

Combined fund balances grew steadily from 2017 to 2020, peaking near $19.3M. The board then lowered its minimum reserve requirement, freeing up $4.9M for building projects. That cushion was spent down on facilities while salaries simultaneously exploded — a double hit that dropped reserves 60% by 2026.
Dashed line = 30% of revenue (board-required minimum reserve)
Exhibit D

The Two Drivers

The two forces that drained TRL's reserves, measured against revenue. Salaries are the structural ramp — from 51 cents of every revenue dollar in 2017 to 59 cents in 2026. Building projects were the temporary spike, peaking in 2024 when the Mountain View library build consumed 11% of revenue. Together they explain the fund balance collapse shown in Exhibit C.

Exhibit E

Fewer People, More Money

TRL's budgeted workforce shrank from 253 full-time-equivalent positions (FTE) in 2017 to 230 in 2025 — a 9% decline. During the same period, total salary expenditures grew 34%, from $11.7M to $15.6M. The entire salary increase was per-person cost growth, not headcount. TRL hired 133 people since 2019, but that was turnover replacement — total positions went down.

Exhibit F

The Structural Mismatch

Revenue is structurally capped by Washington's 1% property tax lid — a state law that limits annual levy increases to 1% regardless of inflation — and cannot keep pace with rising costs. Per-employee salary costs tracked Seattle-area inflation through 2023, then pulled sharply ahead via union contract increases, job reclassifications (upgrading position titles and pay grades), and executive raises. The growing gap between costs and revenue capacity is the crisis leadership never addressed.

Exhibit G

The Revenue Option Never Pursued

TRL's levy rate has declined 45% since 2016 and sits at less than half the statutory maximum. The last levy lid lift — a ballot measure asking voters to approve a higher rate — was attempted in 2009. Heywood's job description requires her to "explore and propose to the Board new potential sources of finance."
